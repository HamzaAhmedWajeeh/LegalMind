LEGAL TERMS GLOSSARY — TECHNOLOGY CONTRACTS

This glossary defines key terms commonly encountered in technology services
agreements, software licences, and related commercial contracts. It is
intended as a reference guide for legal and commercial teams.


INDEMNIFICATION

Indemnification is a contractual obligation by which one party (the indemnitor)
agrees to compensate the other party (the indemnitee) for specified losses,
damages, or liabilities. In technology contracts, indemnification clauses
typically cover: (a) intellectual property infringement claims; (b) breach
of contract; (c) gross negligence or wilful misconduct; and (d) data breaches.

Liability caps on indemnification are standard in commercial contracts and
usually expressed as a multiple of fees paid (e.g., 12 months, 24 months)
or a fixed monetary amount. Mutual indemnification provisions require both
parties to indemnify each other for their respective breaches.

Key distinction: Indemnification covers third-party claims brought against
the indemnitee, while direct damages provisions govern claims between
the contracting parties themselves.


CONSEQUENTIAL DAMAGES

Consequential damages (also called special or indirect damages) are losses
that do not flow directly from a breach but arise from special circumstances
known to the breaching party at the time of contracting. Examples include
lost profits, loss of business opportunity, reputational harm, and business
interruption costs.

Most commercial technology contracts exclude consequential damages through
a "consequential damages exclusion" or "limitation of liability" clause.
These exclusions are enforced in most jurisdictions but may be subject to
carve-outs for: data breaches, fraud, wilful misconduct, and indemnification
obligations.

The enforceability of consequential damages exclusions varies by jurisdiction.
Under English law, such clauses are generally enforceable unless unreasonable
under the Unfair Contract Terms Act 1977.


FORCE MAJEURE

Force majeure (French: "superior force") is a clause that excuses a party
from performance obligations when extraordinary events or circumstances
beyond its reasonable control prevent performance. Common force majeure
events include: natural disasters, acts of war or terrorism, pandemics,
government actions, and critical infrastructure failures.

Three requirements must typically be met: (1) the event must be unforeseeable
or unavoidable; (2) it must be beyond the party's reasonable control; and
(3) it must actually prevent performance rather than merely making it more
difficult or expensive.

Force majeure clauses do not typically excuse payment obligations already
due. Parties relying on force majeure must usually provide prompt notice
and make reasonable efforts to mitigate the impact.


INTELLECTUAL PROPERTY — WORK FOR HIRE

In technology contracts, intellectual property ownership of custom-developed
software and deliverables is governed by the work-for-hire doctrine or express
assignment provisions. Under US law, work created by an employee within the
scope of employment is automatically a "work made for hire" owned by the
employer. Work created by independent contractors requires an express written
assignment to transfer ownership.

"Background IP" or "Pre-existing IP" refers to intellectual property owned
by a party prior to the contract. Vendors routinely retain background IP,
licensing it to clients under limited-use licences. "Foreground IP" refers to
IP created specifically for the client during the engagement.


ARBITRATION CLAUSE

An arbitration clause is a provision requiring parties to resolve disputes
through private arbitration rather than litigation in court. Arbitration
is generally faster and more confidential than litigation. Key elements of
an arbitration clause include: (a) the administering body (e.g., ICC, AAA,
LCIA); (b) the number of arbitrators; (c) the seat of arbitration; (d) the
governing language; and (e) the governing law.

In international contracts, arbitration is often preferred because arbitral
awards are more easily enforced across borders under the 1958 New York
Convention, to which over 170 countries are signatories.


SLA — SERVICE LEVEL AGREEMENT

A Service Level Agreement defines the specific performance standards a
vendor must maintain, the measurement methodology, and the remedies
available to the client if standards are not met. Common SLA metrics include:
system uptime (e.g., 99.9%), response time, resolution time, and data
recovery time objectives (RTO) and recovery point objectives (RPO).

SLA credits are the typical remedy for SLA failures — the vendor credits
the client's account a percentage of monthly fees. SLA credits are generally
the client's sole remedy for SLA breaches and do not constitute a right to
terminate unless persistent failures trigger a separate termination right.


LIMITATION OF LIABILITY

A limitation of liability clause caps the maximum amount either party can
recover from the other in connection with the contract. Caps are typically
expressed as: (a) a fixed amount (e.g., USD $1,000,000); (b) a multiple of
annual or monthly fees; or (c) the amount paid under the contract in a
specific period preceding the claim.

Common carve-outs (amounts NOT subject to the cap) include: fraud,
wilful misconduct, data breach, death or personal injury, and
intellectual property indemnification obligations.

Courts in most common law jurisdictions will enforce limitation of liability
clauses between commercial parties of equal bargaining power, provided the
clause was clearly communicated and does not exclude liability for fraud.


TERMINATION FOR CONVENIENCE

A termination for convenience clause allows one or both parties to end the
contract without cause, subject to advance notice (typically 30 to 90 days).
Upon such termination, the client is generally obligated to pay for all
services rendered and approved expenses incurred up to the termination date.

Termination for convenience clauses are distinguished from termination for
cause clauses, which require a material breach and usually provide a cure
period (typically 30 days). The availability of termination for convenience
significantly affects the risk profile of a long-term services engagement.


DATA PROCESSING AGREEMENT (DPA)

A Data Processing Agreement is a contract between a data controller and
a data processor that governs the processing of personal data. DPAs are
required under GDPR (Article 28) and similar data protection laws whenever
a vendor processes personal data on behalf of a client. Key provisions
include: the subject matter and duration of processing; the nature and
purpose of processing; the type of personal data; the obligations of the
processor; and sub-processing restrictions.

Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs) are pre-approved DPA templates issued
by the European Commission for data transfers outside the European Economic
Area.
